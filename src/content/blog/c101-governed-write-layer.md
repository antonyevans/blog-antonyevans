For weeks my company mind could read itself but not change itself. Today I let it write back, and the hard part wasn't the writing. It was deciding what it's allowed to change without me.

The read layer came first. My AI navigates the mind the way a person would, moving through structure instead of searching by similarity. That was the easy half. A mind you can only read is half a mind. Real operating knowledge doesn't sit still: a decision gets made, a number goes stale, the morning brief learns something the system should keep.

So the AI needs to write. Left ungoverned, that's a liability: one bad patch away from breaking something that mattered.

Every write lands in a zone, and every zone carries its own posture. The AI merges some zones on its own. Others need a second model to check the patch before it lands. A few are off limits to anything but me. Nothing changes silently. I get a receipt for every write, and I can veto it. Whatever reviews a change is never the model that wrote it.

That separation is the whole design. It's easy to get excited about an AI that keeps your knowledge current on its own. The discipline is refusing to let it do that ungoverned. Owning your mind means deciding how it's allowed to change. Reading it is the easy part.

If you let an AI edit your company's knowledge, where's the line it should never cross without you?
